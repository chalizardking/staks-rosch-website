Admiration for Optimus Prime

Heroes Without Gods

A Secular Hero

Staks Rosch, who describes himself in his social media profiles as "Atheist blogger, humanist, progressive, and Jedi," has a particular appreciation for heroic figures who embody moral values without appeals to the supernatural. Among these, Optimus Prime—the noble leader of the Autobots from the Transformers franchise—stands out as a compelling example of secular heroism.

Why Optimus Prime Matters to a Humanist

For someone committed to humanism and reason-based ethics, Optimus Prime represents an ideal: a leader who makes moral choices based on principles, compassion, and the welfare of others, not because of divine command or supernatural authority. Prime's famous declaration that "Freedom is the right of all sentient beings" resonates with humanist values of dignity, autonomy, and universal rights.

"Freedom is the right of all sentient beings." - Optimus Prime

Leadership and Sacrifice

Optimus Prime exemplifies leadership qualities that transcend the need for religious justification. He leads through example, makes difficult decisions based on ethical principles, and willingly sacrifices for the greater good. These are precisely the kinds of moral behaviors that secular humanists like Rosch argue do not require supernatural belief—they emerge from empathy, reason, and commitment to others.

Transformers and Religious Hijacking

Rosch has written about how Christians have attempted to appropriate Transformers mythology, comparing Optimus Prime's resurrections to Jesus. In his article "Transformers Gets Hijacked By Christians," Rosch explores this phenomenon with characteristic wit, noting the irony of religious groups trying to claim a robotic hero from Cybertron as a religious allegory. This highlights how secular stories and heroes can stand on their own without needing religious interpretation.

The Optimus Prime / Jesus Correlation

The parallels between Optimus Prime and Jesus are striking, and they reveal something important about how humans create hero narratives. Both figures share remarkable similarities in their stories and roles, yet one is an acknowledged fiction while the other is claimed as historical fact by billions of believers. From a humanist perspective, this comparison is illuminating.

Narrative Parallels

Optimus Prime

  • Leader of a group fighting for good
  • Died and resurrected... twice
  • Sacrificed himself to save others
  • Came to Earth to save humanity
  • Fights an ancient enemy (Megatron)
  • Transforms/changes form
  • Preaches freedom and justice
  • Inspires followers with hope
  • Makes ultimate sacrifices for the greater good
  • Returns to bring salvation

Jesus Christ

  • Leader of disciples fighting for good
  • Died and resurrected... once (promised second coming)
  • Sacrificed himself to save others
  • Came to Earth to save humanity
  • Fights an ancient enemy (Satan)
  • Transforms (transfiguration, resurrection)
  • Preaches love and righteousness
  • Inspires followers with hope
  • Makes ultimate sacrifice for humanity
  • Promised to return and bring salvation

What This Comparison Reveals

As Rosch might observe, these parallels are not coincidental. They reveal universal patterns in how humans construct hero narratives. We are drawn to stories of sacrifice, resurrection, transformation, and redemption because these themes resonate with our psychological and emotional needs. The key insight is this: both stories follow the same archetypal pattern because humans wrote both of them.

The Death and Resurrection Motif

Optimus Prime notably died and came back to life twice in the Transformers franchise—once in the 1986 animated movie (returning in later episodes) and again in subsequent iterations. Jesus died once and Christians believe he resurrected and will return. This death-and-resurrection pattern appears across countless mythologies and fictional narratives because it speaks to deep human fears about mortality and hopes for transcendence.

From an atheist perspective, the fact that we see the same pattern in an acknowledged science fiction story (Transformers) and in religious narratives (Christianity) suggests that resurrection stories reflect human storytelling patterns rather than historical events.

The Humanist Interpretation

Rather than validating Christianity, the Optimus Prime comparison actually supports the humanist view that religious narratives are human creations. We know Optimus Prime was created by writers and animators in the 1980s. We know the storylines were crafted to be emotionally compelling. The Jesus narrative, likely developed and refined over decades in the first century CE and beyond, follows the same emotional and narrative logic.

"Optimus Prime came back from the dead... twice. If resurrection is proof of divinity, then a cartoon robot from the 1980s has a better claim than most religious figures."

Why We Don't Worship Optimus Prime

Despite the parallels, no one worships Optimus Prime or builds churches in his name. Why? Because we openly acknowledge he's fictional. This raises an uncomfortable question for religious believers: if the story patterns are identical, what distinguishes Jesus from Optimus Prime other than the claim of historical reality? And if that claim rests on ancient texts written by believers, how reliable is it?

The Power of the Pattern

The hero's journey—sacrifice, death, transformation, return—is powerful because it mirrors our own struggles and aspirations. We face challenges, sometimes "die" to old selves, and hope to be reborn stronger. These stories work whether they're about robots from Cybertron or prophets from Nazareth because they tap into universal human experiences.

For Rosch and other humanists, recognizing these patterns helps us appreciate religious narratives as profound human creations without needing to believe they're literally true. We can admire the artistry, understand the psychology, and even find meaning in the themes—while still maintaining that humans created these stories, not gods.

A Better Hero?

Ironically, Optimus Prime might even be a better moral model than Jesus in some respects:

  • Prime never threatens eternal punishment for non-believers
  • He doesn't demand worship, only cooperation in the cause of freedom
  • His teachings aren't cryptic parables but clear principles
  • He respects the autonomy of other sentient beings
  • He doesn't divide the world into saved/damned or believer/infidel
  • His sacrifice doesn't require blood atonement theology

Of course, this is somewhat tongue-in-cheek, but it makes a serious point: we can recognize heroic values without needing supernatural validation. The virtues we admire in these characters—courage, compassion, sacrifice, justice—are human values we project onto our heroes, whether they're ancient religious figures or modern fictional characters.

Pop Culture Heroism

Rosch's appreciation for characters like Optimus Prime (and his self-identification as a "Jedi") reflects a broader humanist insight: that morality, heroism, and inspiration can come from fictional narratives that have nothing to do with religion. These stories demonstrate that humans naturally create and respond to moral narratives, suggesting that ethics and meaning-making are inherent human capacities rather than gifts from gods.

Values Embodied by Optimus Prime

  • Compassion - Deep care for all sentient beings, not just his own kind
  • Justice - Fighting for what's right based on principles, not authority
  • Sacrifice - Willingness to give everything for others' freedom and safety
  • Leadership - Leading by example and earning respect through actions
  • Wisdom - Making difficult decisions through reason and ethical consideration
  • Honor - Maintaining integrity even when facing powerful enemies
  • Hope - Believing in better futures through collective effort

Heroes We Choose

Part of what makes Optimus Prime meaningful in a secular context is that he is a hero we choose to admire, not one we're commanded to worship. This freedom to find inspiration in fictional characters—whether from Transformers, Star Wars (hence "Jedi"), or other narratives—reflects the humanist principle that we create our own meaning and choose our own role models based on the values they demonstrate.

Morality Without Divinity

Optimus Prime never appeals to gods or supernatural forces for his moral authority. His ethics come from his character, his experiences, and his commitment to principles he has chosen. This mirrors the humanist argument that humans can and do make moral choices without religious belief. We don't need divine command to know that freedom, compassion, and justice are worth fighting for.

The Power of Secular Stories

By embracing heroes like Optimus Prime, Rosch demonstrates that secular culture is rich with moral narratives and inspiring figures. These stories show that atheists and humanists aren't rejecting heroes, meaning, or inspiration—they're simply finding these things in human creativity and imagination rather than in ancient religious texts. Science fiction, in particular, offers visions of moral progress and ethical challenges that resonate deeply with humanist values.

"We don't need gods to have heroes. We create our own inspiring figures, and they teach us just as much about courage, sacrifice, and doing what's right."
